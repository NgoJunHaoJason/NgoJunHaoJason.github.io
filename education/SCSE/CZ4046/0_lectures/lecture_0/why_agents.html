Why Agents?

trends

5 ongoing trends (increasing) have marked the history of computing:

  • ubiquity
  • interconnection
  • intelligence
  • delegation
  • human-orientation

ubiquity (pervasiveness)

The continual reduction in cost of computing capability has made it possible to introduce processing power into places and devices that would have once been uneconomic.

As processing capability spreads, sophistication (and intelligence of sort) becomes ubiquitous.

interconnection

Computer systems today no longer stand alone, but are networked into large distributed systems.

Since distributed and concurrent systems have become the norm, some researchers are putting forward theoretical models that portray computing as primarily a process of interaction.

intelligence

The complexity of tasks that we are capable of automating and delegating to computers has grown steadily.

delegation

Computers are doing more for us - without our intervention (ties in with intelligence).

We are giving control to computers, even in safety critical tasks.

human-orientation

We are moving away from machine-oriented views of programming, towards concepts and metaphors that more closely reflect the way we ourselves understand the world.

Programmers (and users) relate to machines differently.

Programmers conceptualise and implement software in terms of higher-level (more human-oriented) abstractions.

Programming has passed through:

  • machine code
  • assembly language
  • machine-independent programming languages
  • subroutines
  • procedures and functions
  • abstract data types
  • objects
to agents.

implications

Delegation and intelligence imply the need to build computer systems that can act effectively on our behalf.

  • Computer systems need to act independently.
  • Computer systems need to act in a way that represents our best interests while interacting with other humans or systems.

Interconnection and distribution have become core motifs in computer science.

Interconnection and distribution, coupled with the need for systems to represent our best interests, implies that systems should cooperate and reach agreements (or even compete) with other systems that have different interests, much as we do with people.

These issues were not studied in computer science until recently.

All of these trends have led to the emergence of a new field in computer science: multi-agent systems.

agents

definitions:

  1. An agent is a computer system that is capable of independent action on behalf of its user or owner - it figures out what need to be done to satisfy designated objectives, rather than constantly being told.
  2. An agent is a computer system that is situated in some environment, and is capable of taking autonomous action in the environment to meet its delegated objectives.

There is a lack of standardised definition - agent means many things to many people.

multi-agent systems

A multi-agent system is one that consists of a number of agents, which interact with one another.

In the most general use case, agents will be acting on behalf of users with different goals and motivations

In order to successfully interact, they require the ability to cooperate, coordinate and negotiate with each other, just like people.

This course covers 2 key problems:

  1. (single) agent design - How do we build agents capable of independent, autonomous action, so that they can successfully carry out tasks that we delegate to them?
  2. society design - How do we build agents that are capable of interacting (cooperating, coordinating, negotiating) with other agents in order to successfully carry out those delegated tasks, especially when the other agents cannot be assumed to share the same interests or goals?

While these questions are all addressed in part by other disciplines (notably economics and social sciences), what makes the multi-agent systems field unique is that it emphasises that the agents in question are computational, information processing entities.

the interdisciplinary nature of multi-agent systems

The field of multi-agent systems is influenced and inspired by many other fields:

  • economics
  • philosophy
  • game theory
  • logic
  • ecology
  • social sciences

Being interdisciplinary led to well-founded methodologies being infused into the field.

However, weaknesses arise as well, such as there being many different views as to what the field is about, and different researchers have different visions.

agents as a paradigm for software engineering

Software engineers have derived a progressively better understanding of the characteristics of complexity in software.

It is now widely recognised that interaction is probably the most important single characteristic of complex software.

Over the last two decades, a major computer science research topic has been the development of tools and techniques to model, understand and implement systems in which interaction is the norm.

agents as a tool for understanding human societies

Multi-agent systems provide a novel new tool for simulating societies, which may help shed some light on various kinds of social processes.

differences in comparison to other fields

distributed / concurrent systems:

  • Agents are assumed to be autonomous, capable of making independent decisions - so they need mechanisms to synchronise and coordinate their activites at run time.
  • Agents are / can be self-interested, so their interactions are 'economic' encounters.

artificial intelligence:

  • we do not need to solve all problems of artificial intelligence in order to build really useful agents.
  • Classical artificial intelligence ignored social aspects of agency; these are important parts of intelligent activity in real-world settings.

economics / game theory:

  • insofar as game theory provides descriptive concepts, it does not always tell us how to compute solutions - we are concerned with computational, resource-bounded agents.
  • Some assumptions in economics / game theory (such as rational agent) may not be valid in building artificial agents.

social science:

  • We can draw insights from the study of human societies, but there is no particular reason to believe that artificial societies will be constructed in the same way.

applications of individual agents

Individual / single agents are used in domains where autonomous action is required.

  • Agents (and their physical instantiation in robots) have a role to play in high-risk situations that are unsuitable or impossible for humans.
  • Agents, as personal software assistants, play the role of proactive assistants to users working on some applications.

The degree of autonomy will differ depending on the situation - human intervention may be an alternative, but not always.

applications of multi-agent systems

Multi-agent systems are useful in domains where:

  • control, data, expertise are distributed
  • centralised control is impossible or impractical
  • processing nodes have competing or conflicting viewpoints or objectives
